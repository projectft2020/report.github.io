## Core Concepts

### 1. Intermarket Technical Analysis (John Murphy)

**Reference:** John Murphy's "Intermarket Technical Analysis"

**Core Concept:** Transmission Logic - Observing cross-asset conduction effects and time lags through charts, not setting "break above resistance / break below support" trades.

### 2. Market Pricing Order

**The Hierarchy:**
1. **Bond Market** (Funding Cost) - First to react
2. **Credit Market** (Corporate Default Risk) - Second
3. **Commodities** (Physical Supply/Demand) - Third
4. **Stock Market** (Retail & Passive Capital) - Last

**Example:**
- US long-term Treasury yields spike
- MOVE index (implied volatility) soars
- High-yield bond credit spreads widen (smart money withdrawing)
- But S&P 500 still celebrates, breaks "previous high resistance"
- Traditional technicians shout: "Volume breakout! W-bottom! Go all-in!"
- **Reality:** This is classic intermarket divergence - underlying liquidity pipes are blocked, stock breakout is final inertia冲刺 or maker trap.

### 3. Market Structure Critique

**Modern Market Characteristics:**
- AI algorithms everywhere

**Key Question:** Do traditional static support/resistance lines still have "market psychological significance" in this structure?

**Author's View:** Likely not. Traditional chart patterns may be meaningless in algorithmic-driven markets.

## Critical Insights

### 1. Time Lag Arbitrage

The most important signal isn't what one market is doing, but the **divergence** between markets:

```
Bonds/Credit → Signal
Stocks → Noise (with lag)
```

When credit market pressure finally transmits to stocks, the "strong support line" crashes instantly under macro capital selling pressure.

### 2. Psychological Barriers vs Real Liquidity

**Question:** Do integer psychological barriers (e.g., 20,000 points) have real financial significance?

**Implication:** In algorithmic markets, "psychological barriers" may be human-defined illusions. Real barriers are liquidity constraints, not chart patterns.
