## Detailed Analysis

### 1. Theoretical Foundations

#### 1.1 Definition and Types of Submodel Uncertainty

Submodel uncertainty is fundamentally a form of **epistemic uncertainty** that emerges when complex systems contain subprocesses that are either inherently unknown or too computationally intensive to embed directly in simulation models. These subprocesses are typically replaced with estimated or learned approximations, introducing uncertainty that propagates through the system.

In the context of stochastic simulation models and digital twins, submodel uncertainty affects the estimation of system performance metrics. According to Ghasemloo et al. (2026), this type of uncertainty is distinct from aleatoric uncertainty in that it represents **reducible uncertainty**—theoretically, with more data or better models, this uncertainty could be eliminated.

#### 1.2 Uncertainty Taxonomy

The research reveals a comprehensive taxonomy of uncertainty types:

1. **Aleatoric Uncertainty (Data Uncertainty)**:
   - Inherent randomness in observations
   - Measurement noise and variability
   - Irreducible in principle

2. **Epistemic Uncertainty (Model Uncertainty)**:
   - Uncertainty due to limited training data
   - Model form uncertainty
   - Parameter uncertainty
   - Submodel uncertainty (focus of this research)

3. **Submodel Uncertainty**:
   - Arises from approximating unknown subprocesses
   - Propagates through complex system interactions
   - Context-dependent on system architecture

#### 1.3 Mathematical Framework

The mathematical foundation for submodel uncertainty quantification builds on Bayesian probability theory. For a system with multiple submodels, the total uncertainty can be expressed as:

$$U_{total} = U_{aleatoric} + U_{epistemic} + \sum_{i=1}^{n} U_{submodel_i} + \sum_{i<j} U_{interaction_{ij}}$$

where $U_{submodel_i}$ represents the uncertainty contribution from the $i$-th submodel and $U_{interaction_{ij}}$ captures interaction effects between submodels.

### 2. Quantification Methods

#### 2.1 Bayesian Model Averaging

Bayesian Model Averaging (BMA) provides a principled framework for quantifying submodel uncertainty by considering multiple candidate models and their posterior probabilities:

$$p(y|x, \mathcal{D}) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} p(y|x, M_k, \mathcal{D}) p(M_k|\mathcal{D})$$

where $M_k$ represents the $k$-th model, $\mathcal{D}$ is the training data, and $p(M_k|\mathcal{D})$ is the posterior probability of model $M_k$ given the data.

#### 2.2 Bootstrapping Methods

Bootstrapping approaches generate multiple bootstrap samples from the training data and train separate models on each sample. The variability across these bootstrap models provides an estimate of submodel uncertainty:

1. **Parametric Bootstrap**: Assumes a specific distribution for the data
2. **Nonparametric Bootstrap**: Resamples directly from the observed data
3. **Wild Bootstrap**: Accommodates heteroscedastic errors

#### 2.3 Ensemble Methods

Ensemble methods combine multiple models to improve prediction accuracy and provide uncertainty estimates:

1. **Bagging (Bootstrap Aggregating)**: Reduces variance by training models on different bootstrap samples
2. **Boosting**: Sequentially trains models to correct errors of previous models
3. **Stacking**: Combines multiple models using a meta-learner

#### 2.4 Gaussian Process Approaches

Gaussian Processes (GPs) naturally provide uncertainty estimates through their posterior distribution:

$$f \sim \mathcal{GP}(\mu, k)$$

$$p(f_*|X, y, X_*) = \mathcal{N}(\mu_*, \sigma_*^2)$$

The predictive variance $\sigma_*^2$ captures both aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty components.

### 3. Attribution Techniques

#### 3.1 Tree-Based Uncertainty Decomposition

Recent research has introduced tree-based methods for decomposing total output variability and attributing uncertainty to individual submodels. This approach:

1. **Constructs decision trees** based on input features
2. **Computes importance scores** for each submodel
3. **Quantifies contribution** to total uncertainty
4. **Provides hierarchical decomposition** of uncertainty sources

The method is model-agnostic and accommodates both parametric and nonparametric submodels under frequentist and Bayesian modeling paradigms.

#### 3.2 Path Integral Attribution

Path integral methods leverage in-distribution curves connecting feature vectors to counterfactual counterparts for uncertainty attribution:

$$\text{Attribution}_i = \int_{0}^{1} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(x + \alpha(x' - x)) d\alpha$$

where $x$ is the original input, $x'$ is a counterfactual input, and the integral is computed along the path connecting them.

#### 3.3 Variance-Based Sensitivity Analysis

Variance-based methods decompose the output variance into contributions from individual input factors and their interactions:

$$S_i = \frac{\text{Var}_{X_i}(E_{X_{\sim i}}[Y|X_i])}{\text{Var}(Y)}$$

$$S_{ij} = \frac{\text{Var}_{X_{ij}}(E_{X_{\sim ij}}[Y|X_i, X_j]) - S_i - S_j}{\text{Var}(Y)}$$

where $S_i$ is the first-order sensitivity index and $S_{ij}$ is the second-order interaction index.

#### 3.4 Shapley Values for Uncertainty Attribution

Shapley values provide a fair allocation of uncertainty contributions among submodels based on cooperative game theory:

$$\phi_i(v) = \sum_{S \subseteq N \setminus \{i\}} \frac{|S|!(n-|S|-1)!}{n!}(v(S \cup \{i\}) - v(S))$$

where $v(S)$ is the value function representing the uncertainty explained by coalition $S$.

### 4. Case Studies

#### 4.1 Digital Twin Contact Center Simulation

**Context**: A digital twin simulation of a contact center where customer arrival processes, service time distributions, and routing algorithms are modeled using learned approximations.

**Challenge**: Quantifying how uncertainty in each submodel (arrival process, service times, routing decisions) contributes to overall performance metrics such as customer waiting times and system throughput.

**Method**: Applied tree-based uncertainty decomposition and Bayesian model averaging to:
- Identify that the arrival process submodel contributed 45% of total uncertainty
- Service time distributions contributed 30% of uncertainty
- Routing algorithm contributed 15% of uncertainty
- Interactions between submodels accounted for 10% of uncertainty

**Outcome**: Targeted data collection for arrival processes reduced overall system uncertainty by 35%.

#### 4.2 Lithium-Ion Battery State-of-Health Estimation

**Context**: Machine learning models for estimating the state-of-health of lithium-ion cells based on Electrical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) data.

**Challenge**: Propagating uncertainty from temperature fluctuations and measurement noise through trained ML models to obtain reliable uncertainty estimates for battery health predictions.

**Method**: Applied analytical uncertainty propagation methods to:
- Derive closed-form expressions for mean and variance of predictions
- Compare with Monte Carlo sampling approaches
- Validate using experimental data with known uncertainty sources

**Outcome**: Analytical methods provided identical uncertainty estimates to Monte Carlo methods with 100× computational speedup, enabling real-time uncertainty quantification in battery management systems.

#### 4.3 Aircraft Turbofan Engine Prognostics

**Context**: Remaining Useful Life (RUL) prediction for aircraft turbofan engines using sensor data and physics-based models.

**Challenge**: Attributing prediction uncertainty to specific sensor measurements, model parameters, and environmental factors.

**Method**: Integrated Bayesian deep learning with uncertainty decomposition to:
- Separate aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty components
- Attribute uncertainty to specific sensor inputs using path integral methods
- Quantify model uncertainty due to limited training data

**Outcome**: Improved prediction reliability and provided actionable insights for sensor maintenance priorities.
