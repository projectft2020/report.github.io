Research Summary¶

This report presents a comprehensive analysis of robust stochastic gradient posterior sampling methods, focusing on theoretical frameworks, stochastic gradient techniques, robustness properties, convergence analysis, and practical applications. The research synthesizes recent advances in scalable Bayesian inference methods that maintain robustness to various sources of uncertainty and noise.

Detailed Analysis¶

Theoretical Framework¶

Bayesian Posterior Sampling¶

Bayesian posterior sampling aims to approximate the posterior distribution (p(\theta|D) \propto p(D|\theta)p(\theta)), where (\theta) represents model parameters and (D) denotes observed data. Traditional Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods face computational challenges with large datasets due to the requirement of evaluating gradients over the entire dataset at each iteration.

Stochastic Gradient MCMC¶

Stochastic gradient MCMC methods address scalability by using minibatch gradients. The general form of the update equation is:

[\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \epsilon_t \nabla \log p(\theta_t|D_{i_t}) + \sqrt{2\epsilon_t} \eta_t]

where (\epsilon_t) is the step size, (D_{i_t}) is a minibatch, and (\eta_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)) is injected noise.

Theoretical Foundations¶

Recent theoretical work has established that constant SGD with appropriate tuning can function as an approximate Bayesian posterior inference algorithm. The stationary distribution of SGD with constant learning rate can be matched to a target posterior distribution by minimizing the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the distributions.

Stochastic Gradient Methods¶

Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics (SGLD)¶

SGLD combines stochastic gradient descent with Langevin dynamics:

[\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \epsilon_t \left( \frac{1}{|B|} \sum_{i \in B_t} \nabla \log p(x_i|\theta_t) + \nabla \log p(\theta_t) \right) + \sqrt{2\epsilon_t \beta^{-1}} \eta_t]

where (B_t) is the minibatch at iteration (t), (|B|) is the batch size, and (\beta) is the inverse temperature parameter.

Key Properties: - Asymptotically samples from the true posterior as (\epsilon_t \to 0) - Scalable to large datasets through minibatch sampling - Requires careful tuning of step size schedules

Stochastic Gradient Fisher Scoring (SGFS)¶

SGFS uses the Fisher information matrix as a preconditioner:

[\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \epsilon_t I^{-1}(\theta_t) \nabla \log p(\theta_t|D_{B_t}) + \sqrt{2\epsilon_t \beta^{-1}} \eta_t]

This method leverages the Bayesian Central Limit Theorem to provide normal approximations of the posterior at high mixing rates while maintaining SGLD-like behavior at slower mixing rates.

Robustness Techniques¶

Stochastic Gradient Lattice Random Walk (SGLRW)¶

SGLRW introduces a novel discretization scheme that enhances robustness:

Key Innovation: Unlike conventional SGLD, SGLRW introduces stochastic noise only through the off-diagonal elements of the update covariance matrix. This modification provides:

  1. Greater robustness to minibatch size variations — Performance remains stable across different batch sizes
  2. Asymptotic correctness — Maintains theoretical guarantees while improving practical stability
  3. Heavy-tailed gradient noise tolerance — Performs well in the presence of non-Gaussian gradient noise

Update Equation: [\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \epsilon_t \nabla \log p(\theta_t|D_{B_t}) + \sqrt{2\epsilon_t} L \eta_t]

where (L) is a lower triangular matrix from the lattice-based discretization.

Density Power Divergence (DPD) Methods¶

DPD-based generalized posteriors provide robustness to outliers and model misspecification:

[p_\gamma(\theta|D) \propto p(\theta) \prod_{i=1}^n p(x_i|\theta)^\gamma]

where (\gamma \in (0,1]) is the robustness parameter. When (\gamma = 1), this reduces to the standard posterior; when (\gamma < 1), it downweights the influence of outliers.

Computational Challenge: DPD-based posteriors involve intractable integral terms for general parametric models, making traditional sampling methods infeasible.

Solution: Novel stochastic gradient algorithms combine loss-likelihood bootstrap with specialized optimization techniques for DPD-based estimation.

Convergence Analysis¶

Theoretical Guarantees¶

  1. Asymptotic Correctness: Both SGLD and SGLRW converge to the true posterior distribution as the step size (\epsilon_t \to 0) at an appropriate rate.

  2. Convergence Rates: Under suitable conditions, the Wasserstein distance between the algorithm's stationary distribution and the true posterior is (O(\sqrt{\epsilon})) for step size (\epsilon).

  3. Robustness Bounds: SGLRW provides tighter bounds on approximation errors under minibatch perturbations compared to SGLD.

Error Sources¶

  1. Discretization Error: Arises from the Euler-Maruyama discretization of the continuous-time Langevin diffusion
  2. Minibatch Error: Due to using noisy gradient estimates from data subsets
  3. Approximation Error: From finite-time convergence in practical implementations

Error Decomposition: [|P_T - P_\infty| \leq C_1 \sqrt{\epsilon} + C_2 \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{|B|}} + C_3 e^{-\lambda T}]

where (P_T) is the distribution at time (T), (P_\infty) is the target posterior, (\sigma^2) is the gradient noise variance, and (\lambda) is the mixing rate.

Applications¶

Bayesian Neural Networks¶

Stochastic gradient posterior sampling methods enable scalable Bayesian inference in neural networks:

  • Uncertainty Quantification: Provides predictive uncertainty estimates crucial for safety-critical applications
  • Regularization: Bayesian approach naturally prevents overfitting
  • Model Selection: Enables comparison of models with different architectures using marginal likelihoods

Experimental Results: SGLRW demonstrates superior stability in Bayesian neural network training, particularly in regimes with heavy-tailed gradient noise or variable batch sizes.

Generalized Linear Models¶

For generalized linear models (GLMs), robust stochastic gradient methods provide:

  1. Outlier Resistance: DPD-based methods maintain stable parameter estimates in the presence of outliers
  2. Scalability: Handle large datasets through minibatch processing
  3. Uncertainty Propagation: Full posterior distribution captures parameter uncertainty

High-Dimensional Models¶

In high-dimensional settings, traditional sampling methods become computationally prohibitive. Stochastic gradient methods:

  • Scale to High Dimensions: Efficiently explore high-dimensional parameter spaces
  • Adapt to Structure: Can incorporate problem-specific structure through preconditioning
  • Maintain Robustness: Provide stable performance even when gradients are noisy or ill-conditioned

Advanced Topics¶

Variants and Extensions¶

  1. Preconditioned SGLD: Uses adaptive preconditioners like RMSprop or Adam to improve convergence
  2. Particle SGLD: Combines multiple chains for better exploration of multimodal distributions
  3. Controlled Variance: Techniques to reduce the variance of stochastic gradient estimates

Practical Considerations¶

  1. Step Size Tuning: Critical for performance; often requires careful annealing schedules
  2. Gradient Preprocessing: Standardization and clipping can improve stability
  3. Diagnostics: Essential to monitor convergence and mixing properties
