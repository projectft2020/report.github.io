Detailed Analysis¶

1. Mathematical Foundations¶

1.1 Wiener Chaos Theory¶

Wiener chaos theory, also known as Wiener-Itô chaos expansion, is a fundamental concept in stochastic analysis that provides a systematic way to represent square-integrable random variables as orthogonal series expansions. The theory is based on the following key concepts:

Hermite Polynomials: These form the basis of the chaos expansion. For a Gaussian random variable, the Hermite polynomials constitute an orthogonal system of polynomials.

Multiple Wiener Integrals: The chaos expansion expresses a random variable F as:

F = E[F] + Σₙ E[Iₙ(fₙ)]

where Iₙ(fₙ) is the multiple Wiener integral of order n, and fₙ are deterministic symmetric functions.

Orthogonality: The multiple Wiener integrals of different orders are orthogonal in the L² space, making the chaos expansion particularly useful for computations.

1.2 Martingale Theory¶

Martingales are stochastic processes where the conditional expectation of the next observation, given all prior observations, equals the most recent value. Key properties include:

  • Discrete-time martingale: A sequence X₁, X₂, X₃, ... where E[Xₙ₊₁|X₁, ..., Xₙ] = Xₙ
  • Continuous-time martingale: A stochastic process Xₜ where E[Xₜ|ℱₛ] = Xₛ for all s ≤ t
  • Martingale property: The process has no predictable drift, making it ideal for modeling fair games and efficient markets

1.3 Stochastic Calculus Framework¶

The integration of Wiener chaos with martingale theory requires the machinery of stochastic calculus:

Itô Calculus: Extends classical calculus to stochastic processes like Brownian motion. Key features include: - Itô integral with respect to Brownian motion - Itô's lemma (change of variables formula) - Integration by parts with quadratic covariation terms

Malliavin Calculus: Extends calculus of variations to stochastic processes, enabling: - Differentiation of random variables - Integration by parts with stochastic functionals - Regularity results for solutions of stochastic differential equations

2. Theoretical Results¶

2.1 Martingale Representation Theorems¶

The fundamental connection between Wiener chaos and martingale representation is established through the following theorems:

Clark-Ocone Theorem: For a square-integrable random variable F satisfying appropriate smoothness conditions:

F = E[F] + ∫₀ᵀ E[DₜF|ℱₜ] dBₜ

where DₜF is the Malliavin derivative of F, and Bₜ is the underlying Brownian motion.

Martingale Representation Property: Any square-integrable martingale Mₜ adapted to the filtration generated by a Brownian motion Bₜ can be represented as:

Mₜ = M₀ + ∫₀ᵗ Hₛ dBₛ

for some adapted process Hₛ.

2.2 Wiener Chaos Decomposition¶

The chaos decomposition theorem states that any L² random variable F can be uniquely represented as:

F = Σₙ₌₀^∞ Iₙ(fₙ)

where: - I₀(f₀) = E[F] (constant term) - Iₙ(fₙ) represents the n-th chaos component - The series converges in L² norm

2.3 Orthogonality and Isometry¶

Key properties that make Wiener chaos expansions computationally tractable:

Orthogonality: E[Iₘ(fₘ)Iₙ(fₙ)] = 0 for m ≠ n Itô Isometry: E[(Iₙ(fₙ))²] = n!‖fₙ‖²ₗ²

These properties are essential for variance computations and statistical analysis.

3. Computational Methods¶

3.1 Chaos Expansion Algorithms¶

Several computational approaches exist for constructing Wiener chaos expansions:

Direct Method: Uses the explicit formula for multiple Wiener integrals and Hermite polynomial expansions.

Stein's Method: Provides bounds on approximation errors and is useful for normal approximations.

Spectral Methods: Employ truncation of the chaos expansion at a finite order for numerical computations.

3.2 Malliavin Derivative Computations¶

The Malliavin derivative DₜF can be computed using several approaches:

Chain Rule: For smooth functionals of stochastic processes, the Malliavin derivative satisfies a stochastic chain rule.

Integration by Parts: The fundamental formula:

E[DF · G] = E[F · δ(G)]

where δ is the Skorokhod integral (adjoint of D).

Finite Difference Approximations: Can be used for numerical computation of Malliavin derivatives.

3.3 Numerical Implementation¶

Practical computational considerations:

Truncation: In practice, chaos expansions are truncated at a finite order N.

F ≈ Σₙ₌₀ᴺ Iₙ(fₙ)

Sparse Grids: For high-dimensional problems, sparse grids can reduce computational complexity.

Monte Carlo Simulation: Combined with chaos expansions for efficient variance reduction.

4. Applications in Finance¶

4.1 Option Pricing and Greeks Computation¶

Wiener chaos expansions are particularly useful in mathematical finance:

Option Pricing: The price of a European option can be expressed as:

C(0,S₀) = E[e⁻ʳᵀ (Sₜ - K)⁺|ℱ₀]

where the chaos expansion of the payoff can be computed analytically.

Greeks Calculation: Malliavin calculus provides an alternative to finite differences for computing sensitivities:

∂V/∂σ = E[VDₜX]  (Malliavin approach)

where X is the underlying process and V is the option value.

Consider the stock price process:

dSₜ = μSₜ dt + σSₜ dBₜ

The chaos expansion of Sₜ can be computed explicitly:

Sₜ = S₀ exp((μ - σ²/2)t + σBₜ)

This can be expanded using the exponential series and properties of Hermite polynomials.
