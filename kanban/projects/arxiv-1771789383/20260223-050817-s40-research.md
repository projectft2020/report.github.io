## Detailed Analysis

### M-Estimation Theory

M-estimators represent a fundamental class of statistical estimators defined as minimizers of objective functions that are sample averages. The "M" denotes "maximum likelihood-type," as these estimators generalize the maximum likelihood principle. Formally, an M-estimator minimizes:

$$\sum_{i=1}^n \rho(X_i, \theta)$$

where $\rho$ is a function that determines the estimator's properties, and $\theta$ represents the parameters to be estimated.

**Key Properties:**
- **Robustness**: M-estimators can be designed to be robust against outliers by choosing appropriate $\rho$ functions that limit the influence of extreme observations
- **Flexibility**: They encompass various estimation techniques including maximum likelihood ($\rho = -\log f$) and least squares ($\rho = (y - X\beta)^2$)
- **Asymptotic Normality**: Under regularity conditions, M-estimators are asymptotically normally distributed, enabling valid inference
- **Consistency**: M-estimators are consistent under uniform convergence conditions and well-separated maxima

The influence function of an M-estimator of $\psi$-type is proportional to its defining function, providing a measure of how individual observations affect the estimator. This property is crucial for understanding the estimator's robustness characteristics.

### Two-Phase Sampling

Two-phase sampling is a cost-efficient strategy where data collection occurs in two stages:

**Phase 1**: Inexpensive variables are measured on a large cohort of size $N$
**Phase 2**: Expensive variables of interest are measured on a strategically selected subsample of size $n$

The mathematical framework involves:
- Let $Y$ denote the outcome variable
- Let $Z$ denote inexpensive covariates
- Let $A$ denote auxiliary variables
- Let $X$ be the expensive variable of primary interest
- Let $R_i$ indicate selection in phase 2 with probability $\pi_i = P(R_i = 1|Z,A,Y)$

**Design-Based vs. Model-Based Approaches:**
- **Design-based estimators** rely on weighted likelihood methods where weights are inverse sampling probabilities ($w_i = 1/\pi_i$)
- **Model-based estimators** assume correct outcome model specification and can achieve higher efficiency but lack robustness to model misspecification

**Neyman Allocation**: The optimal sampling strategy in two-phase designs minimizes the variance of parameter estimates. For $H$ strata, the optimal allocation is:

$$n_i = n \frac{N_i \sigma_i}{\sum_{h=1}^H N_h \sigma_h}$$

where $n_i$ and $N_i$ are phase-2 and phase-1 sample sizes for stratum $i$, and $\sigma_i$ is the population standard deviation.

### Multiwave Methods

Multiwave sampling extends two-phase sampling by dividing phase 2 into multiple waves or subphases, allowing adaptive learning and improvement of the sampling design:

**Wave Structure:**
1. **Wave 1**: Initial sampling based on prior information and phase-1 data
2. **Subsequent Waves**: Adaptive sampling using information accumulated from previous waves
3. **Final Analysis**: Generalized raking combining all collected information

**Influence Function-Based Allocation**: Regression parameters can be expressed as totals of influence functions:

$$N(\hat{\beta} - \beta_0) = \sum_{i=1}^N h_i(\beta) + o_p(N^{-1/2})$$

The optimal multiwave allocation uses the standard deviation of influence functions:

$$n_i = n \frac{N_i \sqrt{\text{Var}(h_i(\beta))}}{\sum_{h=1}^H N_h \sqrt{\text{Var}(h_h(\beta))}}$$

**Prior Information Integration**: Multiwave sampling can incorporate informative priors to improve efficiency:
- Combine priors with phase-1 data to compute posterior distributions
- Use posterior distributions to estimate influence functions
- Derive optimal wave-1 allocations using integer-valued Neyman allocation
- Update priors using posterior distributions from previous waves

### Theoretical Properties

**Asymptotic Theory**: Recent advances establish key theoretical properties for M-estimation under two-phase multiwave sampling:

1. **Asymptotic Linearity**: The estimator can be expressed as a linear approximation plus a remainder term that converges to zero
2. **Asymptotic Normality**: The estimator follows a normal distribution in large samples, enabling valid inference
3. **Consistency**: Under regularity conditions, the estimator converges in probability to the true parameter value
4. **Efficiency**: Multiwave designs approach the efficiency of optimal designs that would require knowledge of unobserved variables

**Generalized Raking**: This calibration technique improves efficiency by adjusting weights so that estimated population totals of auxiliary variables equal true population totals:

$$\sum_{i=1}^N R_i w_i^* S_i = \sum_{i=1}^N S_i$$

where $w_i^* = g_i/\pi_i$ are calibrated weights and $S_i$ are auxiliary variables.

**Bias-Variance Trade-off**: The use of priors introduces a bias-variance trade-off:
- Strong, well-calibrated priors lead to less variable designs but risk bias if priors are misspecified
- Weaker priors result in more variable designs but reduced bias sensitivity
- Weakly informative priors are generally recommended as they provide regularization without excessive bias

### Applications

**Epidemiological Studies**: Multiwave sampling has been successfully applied in large-scale epidemiological research where expensive biomarkers or genetic measurements must be strategically allocated. The National Wilms' Tumor Study demonstrated significant efficiency gains when central lab histology measurements were adaptively sampled.

**Biomedical Research**: In studies with error-prone electronic health records, multiwave validation sampling can efficiently allocate resources for chart validation and data quality assessment.

**Survey Sampling**: National surveys use two-phase designs to collect detailed information on subsamples when comprehensive data collection is prohibitively expensive.

**Prediction-Powered Inference**: Recent advances combine machine learning predictions with expensive measurements to improve efficiency. The Multiwave Predict-Then-Debias estimator specifically leverages proxy information (such as predictions from pretrained models) to enhance estimation while removing bias.

**Efficiency Gains**: Simulation studies demonstrate that multiwave adaptive sampling with Neyman allocation of influence functions yields more efficient estimates than traditional case-control sampling, with empirical relative efficiency often exceeding 90% compared to theoretically optimal designs.
