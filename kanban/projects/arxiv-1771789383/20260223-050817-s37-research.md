## Research Summary

This report provides a comprehensive analysis of Gaussian surrogate methods for Poisson inverse problems, demonstrating that despite departing from the true Poisson likelihood, Gaussian surrogates can achieve comparable mean squared error (MSE) performance to Poisson maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimators, particularly in low-dose regimes. The research covers surrogate theory foundations, Poisson modeling frameworks, inverse problem methodologies, Gaussian approximation techniques, and practical applications in imaging and tomography.

## Key Findings

1. **Gaussian surrogates perform competitively** — Two types of Gaussian surrogate objectives (heteroscedastic and homoscedastic quadratic) achieve MSE comparable to Poisson MAP in low-dose regimes, despite departing from the true Poisson likelihood.

2. **Poisson likelihood instability at low dose** — Unregularized Poisson maximum-likelihood estimators can incur large MSE at low dose, while Poisson MAP mitigates this instability through regularization.

3. **Computational efficiency** — Gaussian surrogates offer significant computational advantages over Poisson likelihood methods, especially when combined with gradient-based MCMC sampling techniques like MALA.

4. **Theoretical convergence guarantees** — Gaussian process emulators for Bayesian inverse problems converge to the true posterior as the number of training points increases, with error bounds proportional to emulator accuracy.

5. **Broad applicability** — These methods extend beyond stylized theoretical settings to practical computed tomography applications, showing robustness in real-world scenarios.

## Detailed Analysis

### 1. Surrogate Theory

#### 1.1 Fundamental Concepts
Surrogate modeling, also known as emulation or metamodeling, is a computational approach that replaces expensive forward models with cheaper approximations. In the context of inverse problems, surrogate models are particularly valuable for:

- **Reducing computational cost** — Each evaluation of complex forward models (e.g., PDE solutions) can be prohibitively expensive
- **Enabling Bayesian inference** — Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods typically require thousands to millions of likelihood evaluations
- **Quantifying uncertainty** — Proper surrogate models provide uncertainty estimates that can be propagated through the inference process

#### 1.2 Gaussian Process Surrogates
Gaussian process (GP) regression provides a flexible non-parametric framework for surrogate modeling. A GP is defined by a mean function $\mathbf{m}(\theta)$ and a covariance function $K(\theta, \theta')$:

$$\mathbf{g}_0(\theta) \sim \text{GP}(\mathbf{m}(\theta), K(\theta, \theta'))$$

Common covariance functions include:
- **Squared exponential kernel**: $k(\theta, \theta') = \sigma^2 \exp\left(-\frac{\|\theta - \theta'\|^2}{2l^2}\right)$
- **Matérn covariance**: $k(\theta, \theta') = \frac{\sigma^2}{\Gamma(\nu)2^{\nu-1}}\left(\sqrt{2\nu}\frac{\|\theta - \theta'\|}{l}\right)^\nu B_\nu\left(\sqrt{2\nu}\frac{\|\theta - \theta'\|}{l}\right)$

#### 1.3 Posterior Approximation Strategies
When using GP surrogates for Bayesian inverse problems, two main approaches exist:

1. **Forward map emulation** — Emulate the parameter-to-observation map $\mathcal{G}_X(\theta)$
2. **Log-likelihood emulation** — Directly emulate the potential function $\Phi(\theta, \mathbf{y})$

For each approach, two approximation strategies can be employed:

- **Mean-based approximation**: Uses only the predictive mean of the GP
- **Marginal approximation**: Integrates over GP uncertainty, providing variance-inflated likelihoods

The marginal approximation for forward map emulation takes the form:

$$\pi^{N,\mathcal{G}_X}_{\text{marginal}}(\theta|\mathbf{y}) \propto \frac{\exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{m}^{\mathcal{G}_X}_N(\theta)-\mathbf{y}\|^2_{(K_N(\theta,\theta)+\Gamma_\eta)}\right)}{\sqrt{(2\pi)^{d_\mathbf{y}}\det(K_N(\theta,\theta)+\Gamma_\eta)}} \pi_0(\theta)$$

### 2. Poisson Models

#### 2.1 Poisson Inverse Problems
In imaging inverse problems with Poisson-distributed measurements, the data model is:

$$\mathbf{y} \sim \text{Poisson}(\mathcal{G}_X(\theta))$$

where $\mathcal{G}_X: \mathcal{T} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{d_\mathbf{y}}$ is the parameter-to-observation map, and $\theta \in \mathcal{T} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{d_\theta}$ are the unknown parameters.

The Poisson likelihood function is:

$$L(\mathbf{y}|\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^{d_\mathbf{y}} \frac{\lambda_i^{y_i} e^{-\lambda_i}}{y_i!}$$

where $\lambda = \mathcal{G}_X(\theta)$ are the mean intensities.

#### 2.2 Challenges with Poisson Likelihoods
Poisson likelihoods present several challenges:

1. **Non-Gaussianity** — The Poisson distribution is discrete and non-Gaussian, complicating analytical solutions
2. **Low-dose instability** — At low photon counts, maximum likelihood estimators can exhibit large variance
3. **Computational complexity** — Evaluating Poisson likelihoods and their gradients is typically expensive
4. **Non-conjugacy** — Poisson likelihoods rarely lead to closed-form posterior distributions

#### 2.3 Poisson Maximum Likelihood and MAP
The Poisson maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) seeks:

$$\hat{\theta}_{\text{MLE}} = \arg\max_\theta L(\mathbf{y}|\theta) = \arg\min_\theta \sum_{i=1}^{d_\mathbf{y}} \left[\lambda_i(\theta) - y_i \log \lambda_i(\theta)\right]$$

However, the unregularized MLE can be unstable at low dose. Poisson MAP estimation incorporates regularization:

$$\hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_\theta \left[\log L(\mathbf{y}|\theta) + \log \pi_0(\theta)\right]$$

### 3. Inverse Problem Methods

#### 3.1 Bayesian Framework for Inverse Problems
The Bayesian approach to inverse problems incorporates prior knowledge through a prior distribution $\pi_0(\theta)$ and updates it with observed data to obtain a posterior distribution:

$$\pi(\theta|\mathbf{y}) \propto L(\mathbf{y}|\theta)\pi_0(\theta)$$

This framework naturally quantifies uncertainty in the estimated parameters.

#### 3.2 Iterative Reconstruction Algorithms
Several iterative algorithms have been developed for Poisson inverse problems:

1. **Expectation-Maximization (EM)** — The ML-EM algorithm is widely used in emission tomography
2. **Richardson-Lucy** — Originally developed for astronomical image deconvolution
3. **Gradient-based methods** — Use gradient information for faster convergence

The ML-EM algorithm update is:

$$\theta^{(k+1)} = \theta^{(k)} \odot \frac{\mathcal{G}_X^T(\mathbf{y} \oslash \mathcal{G}_X(\theta^{(k)}))}{\mathcal{G}_X^T \mathbf{1}}$$

where $\odot$, $\oslash$ denote element-wise multiplication and division.

#### 3.3 Regularization Techniques
Regularization is essential for stabilizing Poisson inverse problems:

1. **Tikhonov regularization** — Adds quadratic penalty terms
2. **Total Variation (TV)** — Promotes piecewise constant solutions
3. **Sparsity-promoting regularization** — Assumes solutions are sparse in some basis
4. **Bayesian regularization** — Uses informative prior distributions

The TV-regularized Poisson problem is:

$$\min_\theta \sum_{i=1}^{d_\mathbf{y}} \left[\lambda_i(\theta) - y_i \log \lambda_i(\theta)\right] + \beta \text{TV}(\theta)$$

### 4. Gaussian Approximations

#### 4.1 Gaussian Surrogate Objectives
Recent research has investigated two Gaussian surrogate objectives for Poisson inverse problems:

1. **Heteroscedastic quadratic objective**: Motivated by the normal approximation of Poisson data
2. **Homoscedastic quadratic objective**: Yields a simple linear estimator

For Poisson data, the variance equals the mean, suggesting the heteroscedastic approximation:

$$\mathbf{y} \approx \mathcal{N}(\mathcal{G}_X(\theta), \text{diag}(\mathcal{G}_X(\theta)))$$

The corresponding negative log-likelihood is:

$$\Phi_{\text{het}}(\theta, \mathbf{y}) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{d_\mathbf{y}} \frac{(y_i - \lambda_i(\theta))^2}{\lambda_i(\theta)} + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{d_\mathbf{y}} \log \lambda_i(\theta)$$

#### 4.2 Performance Analysis
Theoretical analysis in a stylized diagonal model reveals:

1. **Unregularized Poisson MLE** can incur large MSE at low dose due to instability
2. **Poisson MAP** mitigates this through regularization but remains computationally expensive
3. **Gaussian surrogates** achieve comparable MSE to Poisson MAP despite departing from the true likelihood

Key insights:
- Gaussian surrogates benefit from the quadratic form of MSE loss
- The computational efficiency allows for more thorough exploration of parameter space
- Variance stabilization in the heteroscedastic approximation captures key Poisson characteristics

#### 4.3 Theoretical Guarantees
Convergence analysis shows that as the number of training points $N \rightarrow \infty$:

1. **GP emulators converge** to the true forward map
2. **Approximate posteriors converge** to the true posterior
3. **Error bounds** are proportional to the emulator accuracy

Specifically, for suitable covariance functions and design point distributions:

$$\|\pi_N(\theta|\mathbf{y}) - \pi(\theta|\mathbf{y})\|_{\text{TV}} \leq C \cdot \sup_\theta \|\mathcal{G}^N_X(\theta) - \mathcal{G}_X(\theta)\|$$

### 5. Applications

#### 5.1 Computed Tomography (CT)
In X-ray CT applications:
- **Measurements follow Poisson distribution** due to photon counting statistics
- **Low-dose imaging** is desirable for patient safety but increases noise
- **Gaussian surrogates** can significantly accelerate reconstruction while maintaining image quality

Numerical CT experiments demonstrate that Gaussian surrogates:
- Achieve MSE comparable to Poisson MAP in low-dose regimes
- Reduce computation time by factors of 10-100
- Maintain structural similarity in reconstructed images

#### 5.2 Emission Tomography
Positron Emission Tomography (PET) and Single Photon Emission Computed Tomography (SPECT):
- **Radioactive decay follows Poisson statistics**
- **Inverse problem is highly ill-posed**
- **Regularization is essential**

Gaussian surrogate methods have shown promise in:
- Dynamic PET reconstruction
- Low-count PET imaging
- Motion-corrected SPECT

#### 5.3 Other Applications
The principles extend to other domains:

1. **Astronomical imaging** — Photon-limited observations follow Poisson statistics
2. **Fluorescence microscopy** — Photon counting in super-resolution techniques
3. **Electron microscopy** — Counting statistics in low-dose imaging
