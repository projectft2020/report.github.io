Research Summary¶

This research investigates the counterintuitive phenomenon where adding more experts to multi-expert learning systems leads to underfitting and performance degradation. Through analysis of recent research, particularly the paper "When More Experts Hurt: Underfitting in Multi-Expert Learning to Defer," we examine the theoretical foundations, underlying mechanisms, and potential solutions to this paradoxical problem.

Key Findings¶

  1. Multi-expert underfitting is inherent — Unlike single-expert systems where underfitting occurs only under specific conditions, multi-expert systems naturally suffer from underfitting due to expert aggregation effects | Source: arXiv:2602.17144

  2. Expert aggregation term causes label flattening — The mathematical term 𝒜(𝒙) = ∑ⱼ Accⱼ(𝒙) scales with the number of experts (O(J)), flattening label distributions and making optimal labels harder to distinguish | Source: arXiv:2602.17144

  3. Traditional methods fail with multiple experts — Existing surrogate loss methods (Cross-Entropy, One-vs-All) that work well for single-expert scenarios become ineffective as more experts are added | Source: arXiv:2602.17144

  4. PiCCE provides effective mitigation — The proposed "Pick the Confident and Correct Expert" method resolves underfitting by adaptively identifying reliable experts based on empirical evidence | Source: arXiv:2602.17144

Detailed Analysis¶

Multi-Expert Theory¶

Multi-expert systems, particularly Mixture of Experts (MoE), represent a sophisticated form of ensemble learning where multiple expert networks divide a problem space into homogeneous regions. The fundamental architecture consists of:

  • Expert Networks: Specialized sub-models trained on different regions of the input space
  • Gating Network: A routing mechanism that determines which expert(s) to consult for each input
  • Aggregation Mechanism: Combines expert predictions to produce final outputs

The theoretical foundation stems from the principle that different experts can specialize in different aspects of complex problems, potentially leading to better overall performance than any single expert. However, this theoretical advantage assumes optimal learning conditions that are often violated in practice.

Underfitting Mechanisms¶

The research reveals that multi-expert systems suffer from a fundamental underfitting problem that differs significantly from traditional underfitting:

Expert Aggregation Problem¶

The core issue lies in the structure of the multi-expert risk function:

Rℓ_ϕ|𝒙(𝜽) = ∑ᵧ ηᵧ(𝒙)ϕ(𝜽,y) + ∑ⱼ Accⱼ(𝒙)ϕ(𝜽,j+K)

This formulation creates a dummy distribution where class probabilities are normalized by the expert aggregation term:

p̂(y|𝒙) = ηᵧ(𝒙) / (1 + 𝒜(𝒙))
p̂(⊥ⱼ|𝒙) = Accⱼ(𝒙) / (1 + 𝒜(𝒙))

where 𝒜(𝒙) = ∑ⱼ Accⱼ(𝒙) = O(J).

Label Distribution Flattening¶

As the number of experts J increases: - The denominator (1 + 𝒜(𝒙)) grows proportionally to J - Class probabilities become increasingly similar (flattened) - The margin between optimal and suboptimal labels narrows - The classifier struggles to identify the correct label confidently

This effect is particularly problematic because: - It occurs even without deferral costs (unlike single-expert cases) - It scales linearly with the number of experts - It fundamentally limits the learning capacity of the base classifier

Theoretical Bounds¶

The research provides several key theoretical insights:

Risk Decomposition¶

The multi-expert risk can be decomposed as:

Rℓ_ϕ|𝒙(𝜽) = ∑ᵧ ηᵧ(𝒙)ϕ(𝜽,y) + ∑ⱼ Accⱼ(𝒙)ϕ(𝜽,j+K)

This shows that the expert aggregation term 𝒜(𝒙) grows as O(J), creating a fundamental scaling limitation.

Consistency Analysis¶

Under standard assumptions, the research proves that: 1. Classifier Consistency: The base classifier can still learn optimal label predictions 2. System Consistency: Under the "Information Advantage of Optimal Expert" condition, the entire L2D system can achieve Bayes optimality 3. Expert Accuracy Recovery: The method can consistently estimate expert accuracies, crucial for uncertainty quantification

Generalization Bounds¶

The theoretical analysis reveals that: - Without mitigation, generalization error scales with O(J) due to label flattening - With proper mitigation (PiCCE), the error becomes O(1), independent of the number of experts

Experimental Results¶

Performance Degradation with Expert Count¶

Experiments conducted on CIFAR-100 and ImageNet datasets demonstrate clear performance degradation:

CIFAR-100 Results: - 4 experts: 18.48% error (vanilla CE), 18.32% error (PiCCE) - 20 experts: 21.13% error (vanilla CE), 18.11% error (PiCCE) - Performance gap increases from 0.16% to 3.02% as experts grow

ImageNet Results: - 4 experts: 42.74% error (vanilla CE), 42.10% error (PiCCE) - 20 experts: 48.00% error (vanilla CE), 41.32% error (PiCCE) - Performance gap increases from 0.64% to 6.68% as experts grow

Real-World Validation¶

Testing on real-world expert datasets (MiceBone, Chaoyang) confirmed: - The underfitting phenomenon persists beyond synthetic expert scenarios - PiCCE maintains stable performance regardless of expert count - Traditional methods show significant degradation with additional experts

Statistical Significance¶

All experiments were conducted over 3 trials, showing: - Consistent performance degradation for vanilla methods - Statistically significant improvements with PiCCE - Robustness across different base architectures (WideResNet, MobileNet-v2)

Mitigation Strategies¶

PiCCE: Pick the Confident and Correct Expert¶

The proposed solution addresses the fundamental underfitting issue through:

Key Innovation: Instead of considering all experts, PiCCE restricts attention to the subset of experts that are both: 1. Confident: Have high model scores 2. Correct: Have demonstrated empirical correctness (𝟙[mⱼ = y])

Mathematical Formulation:

ℓ̃ᵩ(𝜽,y,𝒎) = ϕ(𝜽,y) + ϕ(𝜽, argmax_{j∈[𝒎=y]} θⱼ₊ₖ + K)

Advantages: - Continuity: Unlike previous approaches, PiCCE maintains continuity essential for gradient-based optimization - Underfitting Resistance: Reduces the expert aggregation term from O(J) to O(1) - Statistical Consistency: Provable guarantees for recovering Bayes optimal solutions

Implementation Guidelines¶

Based on the research findings, effective mitigation strategies include:

  1. Expert Pruning: Remove or deprioritize experts with low accuracy
  2. Confidence Weighting: Use model confidence scores to weight expert contributions
  3. Empirical Filtering: Focus only on experts with demonstrated correctness
  4. Adaptive Aggregation: Dynamically adjust aggregation based on input characteristics

Practical Considerations¶

When implementing multi-expert systems: - Start Small: Begin with fewer experts and gradually increase - Monitor Underfitting: Track classifier performance separately from system performance - Use PiCCE: Implement the proposed loss function for large expert ensembles - Validate Real-World: Test on actual expert data, not just synthetic scenarios
